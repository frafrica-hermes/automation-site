AI Automation Tools Update May 2026

May 15, 2026

Source: https://www.synthesia.io/post/ai-tools Can you believe it’s been over three years since ChatGPT landed in our internet browsers? In a short space of time, AI has become a staple part of daily work and personal life, and the number of AI tools available has grown massively. In the extensive list of AI tools, I’ve pulled the best ones to actually get work done, and that are genuinely useful in 2026. Take a look below. The 12 best AI tools in 2026

  • ChatGPT – to write content, analyze documents, and make videos.
  • Gemini – to compile complex research and make images.
  • Veo – to create AI videos from text or with image prompts.
  • Claude – to write code and automate processes.
  • Grok – to fact check and generate images and videos.
  • NotebookLM – to answer questions using your own documents.
  • Lovable – to build simple web apps from simple prompts.
  • ElevenLabs – to generate realistic voices, text-to-speech, and voice agents.
  • Synthesia – to create business videos and video agents from scripts and documents.
  • Higgsfield – to make inspired dynamic AI images and videos.
  • Gamma – to convert notes into presentations, documents, and webpages.
  • Fathom – to record meetings and create summaries and action items.
  1. ChatGPT Use it for: Writing content, analyzing documents, and making videos. URL: https://chatgpt.com/ ChatGPT is an easy-to-use AI assistant that helps with writing, research analysis, brainstorming ideas, and problem-solving. The tool is particularly good at document analysis – through the chat interface, you can upload PDFs, spreadsheets, screenshots, and other files, and ask it to summarize insights or highlight trends in data. ChatGPT also connects easily to other tools from OpenAI (its parent company), for example, Sora 2 which is an AI video generating tool. Sora 2 can make cinematic, story-driven clips and is one of the more advanced narrative video creation platforms available. ChatGPT’s integration with tools like Sora 2 means you can use it for both creative and administrative tasks, as well as professional and personal projects. You can use ChatGPT with a free account, but there are some usage limits. Paid accounts start at $8 per month.
  2. Gemini Use it for: Search queries, compiling complex research, and making images. URL: https://gemini.google.com/ Gemini is the AI assistant from Google. It’s one of the most popular AI tools because of its multiple uses, including to help with research, writing, data analysis, and multimedia creation. Gemini can handle large amounts of text at once, so if you’re uploading long papers or dense documents, you can ask detailed questions about them and the tool can provide accurate responses that highlight where information from the sources has been pulled. The Deep Research version of Gemini breaks down complex research tasks and creates interactive reports, too. It can even provide reasoning within its customized research reports. For more creative uses, Gemini has the fun-named Nano Banana as its AI image generator and editor, capable of precise edits, background swaps, object additions, and full scene transformations. Meanwhile, the audio overview feature of Gemini converts documents into short, podcast-style summaries, so you can package your content in more diverse ways. Pretty cool, right? There’s a free version of Gemini, but most of the advanced features are in the paid plans, which start from $7.99 per month.
  3. Veo Use it for: Creating AI videos from text or with image prompts. URL: https://labs.google/fx/tools/flow Veo creates short, cinematic video clips from written or visual prompts. You can either upload an image or describe a scene and get a video with realistic lighting, natural motion, and synchronized sound. What makes Veo stand out is that its videos have smooth camera movement and objects aren’t distorted (as is the case for many other AI video generators). The tool is great for building trailers, branded visuals, and mood-driven scenes where realism and atmosphere matter. For businesses, Veo can produce videos for product ads, social media teasers, films, and games. You can access Veo for free, or paid plans begin from $19.99 per month and offer higher quality video production.
  4. Claude Use it for: Writing code and automating processes. URL: https://claude.ai/ Claude is an AI assistant from Anthropic, and is the AI tool of choice for coding and structured reasoning. It’s effective for writing and researching, but is best at supporting development workflows – that’s why its most popular use cases are software development, debugging, refactoring, technical writing, and learning to code. Claude’s strength is writing clean code – typically with fewer logic errors than other AI tools – and being able to explain it in plain English. It can also work directly with large codebases and real projects, instead of just generating snippets of code in the chat. Claude Cowork is available in the app version of Claude, and can carry out tasks across your computer files and tools without you needing to write any code. For people who don’t know how to code, this capability lets you easily set up and automate processes. Like other AI tools, there’s a free version of Claude, but more sophisticated features come with the paid plans, starting at $17 per month.
  5. Grok Use it for: Fact checking and generating images and videos. URL: https://grok.com/ Grok is mostly used as an AI assistant on the social media platform X (formerly Twitter). It’s an AI tool that fact-checks posts and can join online debates to add contextual information or highlight when claims are incorrect or unsupported by evidence. To use Grok, you tag ‘@Grok’ on X posts. The tool then writes and publishes replies in real time and often cites sources to back up what it’s shared. For many people, Grok not only helps reduce misinformation, it makes AI part of everyday conversations on big platforms. Beyond X, Grok has features like ‘Think’ and ‘Deep Search’ to answer queries and write long-form responses. Plus, it can generate images for content ranging from memes to branding components, while Grok Imagine can support fast AI video creation, mostly for short, high-impact clips. Grok’s tiered plans start from free and increase to $300 per year.
  6. NotebookLM Use it for: Answering questions using your own documents. URL: https://notebooklm.google/ If you want to upload, organize, and summarize large amounts of information, NotebookLM is the AI tool for you. You can input PDFs, course materials, notes, and other files and the tool will pull key ideas into clear, concise overviews, as well as answer any questions you have about the files’ content. It’s a brilliant tool to close any knowledge gaps you might have about a subject. An added bonus is that NotebookLM has an audio summary feature that turns files’ content into a podcast-style recap, so you can listen to the information in a more engaging format. Whether studying for exams, reviewing research papers, summarizing documents or getting up to speed on dense topics, NotebookLM is a savvy AI addition. You can use Notebook LM for free, or tiered pricing starts at $8.99 per month.
  7. Lovable Use it for: Building simple web apps from simple prompts. URL: https://lovable.dev/ Loveable is the AI tool for building apps and websites – even if you’re not a coder or designer. Instead of using code, you can use basic tools and interactive widgets to put an app or website together. It’s handy for marketers and entrepreneurs who may not have deep technical knowledge but want to create an online presence or an app. It also saves users time because of its intuitive, straightforward set up. Loveable isn’t for enterprise-grade products, but it’s a powerful starting point for people who are working independently or without development teams. For example, it’s ideal for personal portfolio sites, SaaS-style MVPs, internal tools, and quick prototypes for testing. Loveable is free, or paid access starts from $25 per month.
  8. ElevenLabs Use it for: Generating realistic voices, text-to-speech, and voice agents. ElevenLabs is probably the AI tool that produces the most realistic AI voices from text. It’s commonly used for audiobooks, podcast narration, YouTube voiceovers, customer support, and much more. You just enter what you want the audio to say, choose from different languages, accents, and speakers, and use voice tags like ‘whispers’, ‘sarcastic’, and ‘laughs’ to control the delivery. All the voices sound natural, and there’s the option to clone voices with impressive accuracy. Once trained, your cloned voice can read any text you submit – meaning you can scale audio consistently and quickly. ElevenLabs has Voice Agents too, which are conversational voice bots you can speak to in real time. These can be integrated into apps, customer support systems, call centers, or workflows. There’s a free version of ElevenLabs, while paid accounts range from $5 to $99 per month.
  9. Synthesia Use it for: Creating business videos and video agents from scripts and documents. URL: https://www.synthesia.io/ Synthesia is a one-stop AI tool for business videos and video agents. With it, you can turn prompts, scripts, documents, presentations, and webpages into interactive videos. You can even select and customize an AI avatar to lead the video, naturally guiding viewers through the content. It’s a beneficial tool if you’re working on employee training, onboarding, product explainers, internal communications, multilingual content, and interactive role play learning. Videos from Synthesia don’t require any filming or editing, so non-technical teams can produce high-quality videos in minutes. For the more creative types, Synthesia includes an ‘AI Playground’ where you get free access to Veo 3 and Sora 2 to experiment making B-roll for videos. There are additionally Video Agents in the tool that let viewers interact with videos in real time, which makes content more engaging and is useful for training and simulation videos. The Basic plan from Synthesia is free. Pricing begins at $18 per month.
  10. Higgsfield Use it for: Making inspired dynamic AI images and videos. Higgsfield is an AI tool for creating AI images and videos. It’s also my main creative workspace, as I use it for image and video generation. With the tool, I can work with a wide range of top-tier models inside one clean, professional interface, without constantly switching platforms. For me, the overall experience feels designed for real production work rather than casual experimentation. I also love the combination of unlimited image generation with Higgsfield, and how quickly new models appear inside the tool. I can test new generators almost immediately, reuse reference images, move from image generation to video, and iterate inside the same ecosystem. This saves me a lot of time when I work on short films, advertising content, and creative concepts. In my opinion, Higgsfield AI is one of the most balanced all-in-one platforms available today for serious creative work. It also helps that the huge catalogue of trending AI-generated content from its community keeps me inspired and keeps my content up-to-date. Higgsfield is one of the few AI tools that doesn’t have a free plan. The basic account costs $9 per month.
  11. Gamma Use it for: Converting notes into presentations, documents, and webpages. URL: https://gamma.app/ Gamma is an efficiency-lovers’ dream AI tool. It converts notes into decks, documents, and webpages. To use it, you enter your notes, describe the size and scope of your preferred format, and voila, you’ll have it generated in seconds. You can then edit the deck, document or webpage copy, and export the content if you’ve generated a PowerPoint (.pptx). The decks from Gamma are especially clean and stylish, and come with the option of impressive AI-generated visuals. And, if you’re someone who has to regularly give presentations, you can use Gamma to quickly format professional-looking slides, while you concentrate on refining your ideas. Gamma is available with a free plan, Plus plan ($9 per month), Pro plan ($18 per month) or Ultra plan ($90).
  12. Fathom Use it for: Recording meetings and creating summaries and action items. You’ve probably seen Fathom pop up during your work calls. It’s an AI notetaker that joins Zoom or Microsoft Teams meetings and records the conversation, and it’s one of the most genuinely useful tools I use day-to-day. Fathom makes a full transcript of the call, and afterward produces a structured summary. If I need to be reminded of an action item after a call or double-check what someone said, Fathom has me covered. The notes are always accurate and well-organized in my experience, and I think it captures questions and action items well. For busy professionals, people with packed call calendars, or just those who love to chat, Fathom is one of the top transcript AI tools – sometimes I wonder how I ever worked without it. Fathom is free (and says it will be forever) but paid plans start from $15 per month and have advanced features like CRM sync. 10 Best Secure AI Tools for Business Protection in 2026#

Blog AI Insights 10 Best Secure AI Tools for Business Protection in 2026 AI Insights Published : May 14, 2026 26 Mins Read Summarize this blog post with: ChatGPT Claude Perplexity

  • Discover the best secure AI tools for businesses in 2026 and how they support safer AI adoption.
  • Explore major AI security risks, including prompt injection, data poisoning, model extraction and sensitive data leakage.
  • Learn which features to look for in secure AI tools, from threat detection and guardrails to observability and compliance support.
  • Know how to choose the right secure AI tool by evaluating privacy controls, usability, team oversight and overall cost.

AI is already part of everyday business work, even when there is no formal plan for its secure use. One team member may use ChatGPT to draft content, another may test Claude for research and someone else may use Gemini for analysis. Before long, AI activity is spread across multiple tools, logins, subscriptions and privacy settings. For businesses and agencies, that scattered setup can create real risk. Client notes, website content, sales data, internal plans and sensitive prompts may move through platforms your team does not fully manage or understand. The problem is not just AI use. It is unmanaged AI use. That is why secure AI tools matter in 2026. The right setup can help your team use AI more confidently while reducing risks around data leakage, prompt exposure, weak oversight and tool sprawl. In this blog, we’ll walk through the best secure AI tools for businesses in 2026, what each type of tool helps protect and how to choose the right setup for safer, more productive AI use. Secure AI tools matter more than ever in 2026 because AI adoption is growing faster than most businesses can secure it and traditional cybersecurity tools do not fully address AI-specific risks such as prompt injection, data leakage and model theft. AI is now used across far more business functions than chatbots or content creation alone, with teams relying on generative AI, large language models and autonomous agents for research, writing, coding, customer support, workflow automation and decision-making.

  • As AI becomes more connected to business systems and sensitive data, the risks become more serious and harder to manage.
  • Traditional security tools can protect networks, endpoints and cloud environments, but they are not designed to control how AI models handle prompts.
  • Secure AI tools are built to close that gap by helping businesses protect sensitive inputs, monitor outputs, apply guardrails, manage access and reduce the risk of misuse or exposure.

Some help businesses discover and govern AI usage, some protect prompts and outputs in real time and others secure models, data or AI applications. The best choice depends on where your biggest risk sits, whether that is tool sprawl, sensitive data exposure, shadow AI, model attacks or compliance oversight.

  • Reduce tool sprawl from separate AI subscriptions.
  • Manage team access through a centralized dashboard.

It works as a security layer for teams building or deploying AI apps, especially when those apps interact with users, tools, documents or business systems. Lakera describes Guard as protection against direct and indirect prompt injection, sensitive information leakage and other common LLM security risks. For businesses building customer-facing AI assistants or internal LLM tools, Lakera Guard can help enforce safer behavior at the prompt and response level. It is most relevant for technical teams that need runtime defenses around AI applications, not just a safer interface for everyday AI use. Key features:

  • Detects direct and indirect prompt injection attempts.
  • Helps block jailbreaks and unsafe model behavior.
  • Supports real-time protection for LLM applications.
  • Helps reduce sensitive data leakage risks.
  • Works well for production AI apps and chatbots.

Security and privacy focus: Its main focus is runtime protection for LLM applications, especially around prompt injection, jailbreaks and sensitive data exposure. Ease of use: It is more relevant for technical teams than general business users because it is designed to sit around AI applications and workflows. Best-fit use cases: Customer-facing AI assistants, internal LLM tools, chatbot security, AI app guardrails and real-time prompt/response protection.

Protect AI describes its product suite as covering AI applications from model selection and testing through runtime and beyond. This tool is a better fit for organizations that build, fine-tune or deploy their own AI and machine learning systems.   • Supports AI red teaming and security testing.
  • Helps manage AI security posture across the lifecycle.
  • Protects AI applications from development to runtime.
  • Useful for teams building or deploying custom AI systems.

Security and privacy focus: Its focus is AI lifecycle security, including model scanning, AI security testing, runtime protection and AI security posture management. Ease of use: It is best suited for security, ML and AI engineering teams, rather than everyday business users. Best-fit use cases: Model scanning, AI red teaming, AI security testing, ML pipeline protection and securing custom AI systems.

Its platform includes AI asset discovery, supply chain security, attack simulation and runtime security. This makes it a strong fit for enterprises that need visibility into AI assets, model behavior and emerging attacks. It is especially useful for businesses with production AI systems, internal models or AI applications that need continuous monitoring and protection. Key features:

  • Helps secure the AI supply chain.
  • Supports AI attack simulation and testing.
  • Provides runtime protection for AI systems.

IBM describes it as an enterprise-grade solution for agent monitoring, risk management and regulatory compliance. This tool is best for larger organizations that need responsible AI workflows, explainability, audit trails and oversight across AI projects. It is less about blocking prompt attacks directly and more about making sure AI is governed, documented and aligned with business and regulatory requirements. Key features:

Best-fit use cases: AI lifecycle governance, compliance documentation, AI risk management, responsible AI workflows and enterprise oversight.

Its platform is designed to discover, assess and govern AI assets across an organization, including models, agents, data pipelines, MCP servers and AI-powered tools. Noma solution is built around continuous visibility and risk protection across the AI landscape. This is useful for organizations worried about shadow AI, unmanaged AI assets and risks across complex AI environments. Wiz AI Security uses AI Security Posture Management to help organizations secure AI applications across models, training data and AI services. The key security risks in AI systems include prompt injection attacks, data poisoning, model inversion and extraction, sensitive data leakage and supply chain vulnerabilities. These risks matter because AI systems do not just process code and infrastructure. They also process prompts, training data, model weights, connected tools and user inputs. This creates attack paths that traditional security controls often miss. OWASP now lists prompt injection as a top LLM risk and NIST’s adversarial ML taxonomy covers threats such as poisoning, privacy breaches and supply-chain attacks across the AI lifecycle.

1. Prompt injection attacks#

Prompt injection attacks happen when an attacker crafts input that manipulates a model into ignoring its original instructions or revealing information it should not expose. In practice, this can mean bypassing safety rules, extracting hidden system prompts or tricking an AI app into taking unsafe actions through connected tools or retrieval systems. OWASP warns that prompt injection can cause data leakage, unauthorized actions and unsafe outputs and that common techniques like RAG do not fully solve the problem by themselves. Model jailbreaks are a real-world version of this risk, where users try to override safeguards with carefully written prompts.

2. Data poisoning#

Data poisoning occurs when malicious or low-integrity data is inserted into the training, fine-tuning or retrieval pipeline so the model learns the wrong patterns or produces manipulated results. This can weaken model quality, introduce hidden biases or create backdoors that only activate under certain conditions. NIST identifies poisoning as a core adversarial ML threat and notes that it can affect multiple learning paradigms, making it a serious concern for organizations training custom models or relying on external datasets.

3. Model inversion and extraction#

Model inversion and extraction attacks target the model itself. Inversion attacks try to infer sensitive information about training data from model outputs, while extraction attacks aim to reconstruct the model’s behavior, logic or proprietary value through repeated querying. For businesses, that can mean privacy harm, loss of intellectual property and competitive risk if a model’s unique behavior is copied or probed too deeply. NIST includes privacy breaches and model-targeted attacks in its adversarial ML taxonomy because these risks can expose both data and the model’s underlying value.

4. Sensitive data leakage#

Sensitive data leakage happens when prompts, outputs, logs or chat history expose confidential information such as PII, financial details, internal documents or client data. This is one of the most immediate risks for businesses using AI in everyday workflows. A well-known real-world example came from OpenAI’s March 2023 ChatGPT outage, when some users could see titles from other users’ chat histories, showing how even limited exposure can undermine privacy and trust. In business settings, this same category of risk becomes more serious when employees paste customer records, legal content or proprietary material into AI tools without strong privacy controls.

5. Supply chain vulnerabilities#

Supply chain vulnerabilities affect the broader AI stack, including open-source models, model files, datasets, dependencies, plugins and deployment components. If any part of that chain is compromised organizations may inherit hidden malware, poisoned artifacts, unsafe defaults or manipulated model behavior. NIST specifically calls out AI supply-chain attacks, including risks tied to model technology suppliers and malicious code added upstream. This is especially important for teams adopting open-source models quickly without verifying provenance, integrity and update hygiene. In other words, the best tools do not just give businesses access to AI. They help them use AI with greater security, privacy and accountability.

1. Threat detection for AI models#

One of the most important features in any secure AI tool is the ability to detect threats that are specific to AI systems. This includes identifying unusual prompt patterns, attempted jailbreaks, prompt injection behavior, abnormal model interactions and suspicious access patterns that may signal probing or extraction attempts. Without this layer of detection, businesses may not realize an AI system is being manipulated until harmful outputs, leaked data or unsafe actions have already occurred.

2. Input/output filtering & guardrails#

Effective secure AI tools should also apply strong controls to both what goes into a model and what comes out of it. On the input side, that means screening prompts for malicious instructions, unsafe requests or attempts to override system rules. On the output side, it means checking responses for harmful content, sensitive information, policy violations or hallucinated guidance that could create business or compliance risks.

3. Data encryption & privacy controls#

Data protection is a core requirement, especially for businesses that handle customer data, internal documents, financial information or regulated content. Effective secure AI tools should support encryption in transit and at rest, along with privacy controls that reduce the risk of sensitive data exposure. Depending on the use case, this can include prompt masking, redaction, private processing environments, limited retention settings, access controls and stronger protections around logs and stored conversations.

4. Model monitoring & observability#

AI systems need ongoing visibility, not just one-time setup. Secure AI tools should provide monitoring and observability features that help teams understand how models are being used, what kinds of prompts they are receiving, how they are responding and whether risk patterns are emerging over time. This visibility is critical for spotting misuse, measuring drift in behavior, identifying repeated attack attempts and improving controls as usage expands.

5. Compliance support (GDPR, HIPAA, SOC2)#

For many organizations, AI security also has a compliance dimension. Effective secure AI tools should help support requirements tied to frameworks and regulations such as GDPR, HIPAA and SOC 2. That may include audit logs, role-based access controls, encryption standards, data handling controls, retention policies and administrative oversight. While compliance alone does not guarantee security, tools that are designed with these requirements in mind make it easier for businesses to adopt AI without creating unnecessary legal, operational or reputational risk.   • AI data security tools: AI data security tools protect the information used by AI systems, including prompts, training data, outputs, logs and connected documents.   • AI runtime protection tools: AI runtime protection tools monitor AI systems while they are actively in use. They help detect real-time threats such as prompt injection, jailbreak attempts, suspicious usage patterns, unsafe outputs and policy violations before they cause larger problems.

Many teams focus only on model quality, but the bigger challenges often come from cost, privacy, fragmentation, oversight and day-to-day usability. Here are the areas businesses should pay close attention to when evaluating secure AI tools.

1. Look for tools that reduce AI sprawl#

One of the biggest problems businesses face is fragmentation. Teams often end up using multiple AI platforms for different tasks, which creates extra logins, scattered workflows, inconsistent outputs and unnecessary subscription costs. A strong secure AI tool should help reduce that sprawl by giving teams access to multiple models in one place, making it easier to work across tools without losing control.

2. Prioritize privacy controls#

If your business works with client information, legal documents, healthcare content, financial records,or internal strategy, privacy should be one of the first criteria on your checklist. Not every AI tool is designed for sensitive workflows. Businesses should look for features such as sanitization layers, encrypted prompts, privately hosted model access, zero-retention options and protections against public model training on confidential inputs.

3. Choose tools with administrative oversight#

Many AI platforms are built for individual users, not for businesses managing multiple team members or clients. That creates a problem when companies need visibility into who has access, how seats are assigned or how sensitive conversations should be handled. Businesses should look for secure AI tools that include centralized admin features, seat management, user-level access controls and the ability to apply stricter privacy settings where needed.

4. Watch for inconsistent outputs#

Another practical issue is inconsistency. Different models perform better for different tasks, which means businesses often jump between platforms to get better writing, research, coding or analysis. That slows teams down and makes the workflow harder to manage. A better approach is to choose a secure AI tool that supports multiple models in one environment and allows teams to compare outputs without switching platforms.

5. Balances usability with security#

Security matters, but if a platform is too complicated, teams will find workarounds. The right secure AI tool should feel easy to use while still adding meaningful protections behind the scenes. That includes a familiar chat-style interface, simple onboarding and built-in security features that do not make the workflow harder than it needs to be.

6. Evaluate based on value#

Businesses should also look beyond security alone and ask whether the tool creates operational value. If a team is paying for several premium AI subscriptions, a secure AI platform should ideally help consolidate costs while improving productivity. For SMBs and agencies in particular, that combination can make the difference between scattered AI adoption and a more sustainable long-term workflow. In short, the right secure AI tool for your business should not only protect data and reduce risk. It should also solve practical problems like tool sprawl, weak oversight, privacy concerns, inconsistent model performance and rising subscription costs.   • n8n: 422+ community-built nodes. The smallest catalog, but it includes all major services. The HTTP Request node covers anything without a dedicated integration. Complexity and Learning Curve Zapier: 15 Minutes to Your First Zap Zapier is the tool your marketing manager can use without training. The step-by-step builder holds your hand. Templates cover common use cases. The learning curve is essentially flat for simple automations. The downside: when you need something complex — conditional branching, error handling, data transformation, loops — Zapier’s simplicity becomes a constraint. You end up building workarounds (multiple Zaps chained together) that are harder to maintain than just using a more powerful tool. Make: An Hour to Get Comfortable Make’s visual scenario builder is more powerful than Zapier’s step editor. You see the entire flow as a diagram. Data mapping is explicit — you see exactly which fields flow where. Branching, error handling, and iteration are first-class features. The trade-off: you need to understand how data flows through modules. Non-technical users can learn it, but there’s a genuine 2-3 hour investment before you’re comfortable building multi-step scenarios. n8n: A Weekend to Get Productive n8n is a developer tool that happens to have a visual UI. If you can’t, you’ll be frustrated. The workflow editor is node-based — similar to Make but with more options per node. The Code node lets you write arbitrary JavaScript or Python, which is either liberating or terrifying depending on your background. For teams already building coding agents or working with LLMs programmatically, n8n feels natural. The mental model transfers directly.
